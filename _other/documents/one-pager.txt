The wedge

We sell forecasts that maximise profit, not accuracy. Our parent product, Cassandra, has run print-circulation forecasting for The Washington Post, The New York Times, Chicago Sun-Times, Philadelphia Inquirer, Denver Post and Toronto Star for over a decade. GORM is Cassandra rebuilt on top of the new generation of time-series foundation models — and generalised to any sector that has to decide "how many of X do I put where, tomorrow?"

The problem

Every retailer, publisher, restaurant chain and hospital faces the same daily question: how much of what, where. Get it wrong and you either lose the sale (stock-out) or eat the inventory (waste). Enterprise tools (o9, Blue Yonder, SAP IBP) cost $500k+ and take a year to install. Open-source stacks (Prophet, statsmodels) need a PhD-led data team. The mid-market — the bulk of the global $828M → $2.07B forecasting SaaS spend by 2035 — has no good option.

The product

GORM is a forecasting and decisioning platform with three things competitors don't combine:

1. An ensemble of 20+ frontier AI foundation models — Google TimesFM 2.5, Amazon Chronos 2, Salesforce Moirai 2, IBM FlowState, Datadog Toto, Alibaba YingLong, Tsinghua Sundial, NX-AI TiRex, ShanghaiTech Kairos, plus statistical baselines. Each forecast is a weighted blend, re-selected per series. As new models ship, we add them — the platform gets better without us retraining anything.

2. A profit-optimisation layer (Newsvendor / Economic Optimal) — we fold cost, margin and price directly into the decision via the critical-fractile quantile. High-margin items automatically get larger draws. Output is how many to deliver, not what the model thinks demand is.

3. Natural-language interface — operators ask in plain English ("Sunday circulation, Chicago, Q4"; "expected profit, 7-Eleven Denver, 2H 2024"); GORM returns a forecast, a scenario, or a chart.

Why now

Foundation models for time-series went production-ready in 2024–2026 (TimesFM 2.5, Chronos 2, Moirai 2). What used to be a 12-month custom-model build is now a model-orchestration problem. First mover in the orchestration + decisioning layer wins, the same way Vercel won the hosting layer above Next.js.

Moat

- Proprietary dataset. ~130M sales observations across +40 publications, US/CA/EU, daily resolution including holidays — the right shape to fine-tune any of the foundation models we host. Already shipping a fine-tuned TimesFM variant in production.

- 12 years of operational know-how. What does "good" look like on Thanksgiving in Denver? We know. The Economic Optimal layer, weekday corrections, PAD (holiday/event) adjustments, variation handling — all production-tested.

- Reference customers in a defensible niche. Newsrooms are not glamorous, but they pay, they renew, and they are an extraordinary testbed: high noise, strong seasonality, real money on every decision.
